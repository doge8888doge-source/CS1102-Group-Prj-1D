CS1102 - Course Project - 2025 / 2026 Semester B

Topic: AI-Driven Detection of Synthetic Identity Fraud in Consumer Lending

Real world case studies/example

A sophisticated document fabrication scam recently targeted Mr. Thompson, a Manchester small company owner. He paid a £50,000 down payment and requested £86,000 in financing when he filed for motor credit through a dealer to buy a premium vehicle valued at £135,000. He was unaware that the dealer had filed fake paystubs and bank statements under his name. The application was accepted by the bank, and by the time Mr. Thompson learned of the deception four months later, the payments had already ceased. The examination showed that the bank's manual review procedure had already overlooked eighteen identical fraudulent applications during the preceding year, despite his prompt reporting of the matter to the bank.

Problem

First, fraudulent documents were not detected by the bank's transaction monitoring and document verification systems. The bogus program included digitally modified bank statements with anomalies in font rendering, pixel-level errors, and compression artifacts. The bank only used manual human review, which took 30 to 35 minutes each application and was unable to detect sophisticated forgeries, but an appropriately trained AI document forensics system would have detected these tampering traces right away.

Second, there was an unsecure authentication procedure for papers submitted by dealers. Without examining metadata (such as creation history or editing software used) or cross-referencing document patterns across several programs, the bank only confirmed that documents were available and appeared to be correct on the surface. Because the system lacked a "velocity check" for similar document structures sent within a limited time range, fraudsters were able to repeatedly employ the same counterfeit templates.

Third, there was inadequate interaction between fraud protection systems and customer reporting. The bank's disjointed IT systems were unable to immediately freeze the linked financing agreement or flag related pending applications, despite Mr. Thompson reporting the scam as soon as he learned of it. Mr. Thompson was responsible for the £86,000 loan as the fraudster was able to finish the transaction and vanish with the car due to the delay in detection, reporting, and resolution.

Solution

By using AI-powered document forensics tools (like Resistant AI), which look at how a document was created rather than merely analyzing its content and identify pixel-level manipulation, metadata anomalies, and global pattern mismatches, banks can improve their technological infrastructure. By processing each document in 12 seconds as opposed to 15 minutes, these technologies reduce manual labor and detect forgeries that people would overlook.

Batch forging attacks can also be identified by using velocity checks on document submissions, such as notifying when the same template or metadata signature appears more than three times in a 24-hour period.

Additionally, banks should set up real-time interaction between their transaction/application processing systems and consumer fraud reporting channels. The system should instantly freeze any linked pending applications and flag related identifiers (phone number, address, and email) throughout the whole organization when a customer reports suspected fraud.
